# Project Vision

## Why We Built This

Assistive technology has a fragmentation problem. Tools for the blind assume the user can hear. Tools for the deaf assume the user can see. Tools for the speech-impaired assume the user can do at least one of the two. Almost nothing exists for people who face all three barriers at once.

This isn't a niche edge case — it is a failure of imagination in the industry. We set out to fix it.

## What We Are Building

Lumos X ASL Gloves is not a single product. It is an ecosystem of two complementary systems designed to cover the full spectrum of sensory disability:

**Lumos AI Glasses** targets individuals with a single disability — blindness, deafness, or muteness — and provides a unified AI-powered wearable that adapts to each user's needs without requiring a separate device for each condition.

**ASL Gloves** targets the most underserved group: individuals who are simultaneously blind, deaf, and mute. By communicating entirely through touch and tactile feedback, the gloves bypass every channel that existing technology relies on.

Together, they are designed so that no disability profile falls through the gap.

## Core Principles

**Accessibility should not be fragmented.** A person's combination of disabilities should not determine whether technology exists for them. We design for the hardest case first.

**Independence over dependence.** Every feature we build is measured by whether it reduces reliance on caregivers and interpreters, not just whether it works in a lab.

**Affordability is a feature.** Assistive technology that costs thousands of dollars is not accessible technology. The ASL Gloves bill of materials comes to under ₹2,600. That is a design constraint, not a coincidence.

**Open source by default.** Hardware schematics, firmware, and software are open. Accessibility tools should be improvable by the communities that need them.

## Long-Term Direction

The current prototypes validate the core concept. The roadmap beyond them involves:

- Moving Lumos off cloud-dependent AI (OpenAI, Gemini) toward on-device inference, reducing cost and latency while enabling offline use
- Expanding ASL Gloves to support multiple sign languages beyond ASL
- Miniaturising both products toward commercial form factors
- Building emergency SOS features and haptic notification layers
- Seeking partnerships with disability organisations, hospitals, and schools for field validation

The goal is not just a working prototype — it is a product that someone can buy, wear, and trust with their daily communication.
